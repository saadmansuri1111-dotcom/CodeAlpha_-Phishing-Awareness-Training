What is Phishing?

📖
Phishing is a type of social engineering attack where cybercriminals impersonate trusted entities to trick individuals into revealing sensitive information, clicking malicious links, or downloading harmful attachments.
🎯

The Goal

Attackers aim to steal credentials, financial data, personal information, or install malware on your systems. A single click can compromise an entire organization.

📧

The Method

Phishing typically comes through email, but can also use SMS (smishing), phone calls (vishing), social media, or fake websites to lure victims.

🔬 Anatomy of a Phishing Attack

1. Research

Attacker gathers info about the target

2. Craft

Creates convincing bait (email, page, etc.)

3. Deliver

Sends the phishing message to victims

4. Exploit

Victim clicks link, enters credentials, or opens file

5. Harvest

Attacker collects stolen data or gains access

Types of Phishing Attacks

📧

Email Phishing

Email Phishing

Mass emails impersonating banks, companies, or services. Often contain urgent language and suspicious links. Most common form of phishing.

🎯

Spear Phishing

Click to learn more

Spear Phishing

Highly targeted attacks directed at specific individuals or organizations. Uses personal information to appear legitimate and trustworthy.

🐋

Whaling

Click to learn more

Whaling

Targets high-level executives (CEOs, CFOs). Attackers pose as other executives or legal entities to authorize wire transfers or reveal confidential data.

📱

Smishing (SMS)

Click to learn more

Smishing

Phishing via text messages. Often claims to be from delivery services, banks, or government agencies with urgent action needed.

📞

Vishing (Voice)

Click to learn more

Vishing

Phone-based phishing where attackers call pretending to be tech support, IRS, or your bank. Uses urgency and authority to extract information.

🌐

Clone Phishing

Click to learn more

Clone Phishing

Duplicates a legitimate email you previously received, but replaces attachments or links with malicious versions. Very hard to detect.

Recognizing Phishing Emails

Inbox — 1 New Message
From: security@paypa1-support.com 🚩 Red Flag #1
To: you@company.com
Subject: ⚠️ URGENT: Your Account Has Been Compromised — Immediate Action Required!!! 🚩 Red Flag #2

Dear Valued Costumer, 🚩 Red Flag #3

We have detected suspicous activity on your PayPal account. Your account has been temporarily limited. You must verify your identity within 24 hours or your account will be permanently suspended.

Click the button below to verify your account immediately:

🔓 Verify My Account Now 🚩 Red Flag #4

If you did not make this change, please contact us immediately.
Sincerely,
PayPal Security Team 🚩 Red Flag #5

⚠️

This is a phishing link!

In a real scenario, clicking this could have taken you to a fake login page designed to steal your credentials. Always hover over links to check the real URL before clicking.

✅ Quick Detection Checklist

🔍 Check the sender's email address carefully
⏰ Beware of urgency & threatening language
📝 Look for spelling & grammar errors
🔗 Hover over links before clicking
👤 Watch for generic greetings ("Dear Customer")
📎 Be suspicious of unexpected attachments
04

Spotting Fake Websites

✅ Legitimate
🔒 https://www.paypal.com/signin
  • HTTPS with valid certificate
  • Correct domain name
  • Clean, professional URL structure
VS
❌ Phishing
⚠️ http://paypa1-secure.login-verify.ru/signin
  • HTTP — no encryption
  • Misspelled domain ("paypa1")
  • Suspicious foreign TLD (.ru)
  • Excessive subdomains/hyphens

🎭 Common URL Tricks Attackers Use

Typosquatting
gooogle.com amazom.com

Misspelled versions of real domains

Subdomain Abuse
paypal.com.evil-site.com

Real domain used as subdomain of a fake one

Homograph Attack
аpple.com (Cyrillic 'а')

Using look-alike Unicode characters

URL Shorteners
bit.ly/x8kQ2m

Hides the real destination URL

05

Social Engineering Tactics

Phishing is a form of social engineering — the art of manipulating human psychology rather than exploiting technical vulnerabilities. Here are the key psychological principles attackers exploit:

⏰

Urgency & Scarcity

"Act now or lose your account!" — Creates panic to bypass rational thinking.

"Your account will be deleted in 24 hours unless you verify immediately."
👔

Authority

Impersonating bosses, CEOs, government agencies, or IT departments to command compliance.

"This is the IT department. We need your password to fix a critical security issue."
🤝

Trust & Familiarity

Using known brand logos, colleague names, or personal details to appear legitimate.

"Hi [Your Name], as discussed in our last meeting, please review this document."
😨

Fear

Threatening consequences like legal action, account suspension, or financial loss.

"Unusual login from Russia detected. If this wasn't you, click here immediately."
🎁

Reciprocity & Greed

Offering free gifts, prizes, or exclusive deals to lure victims into clicking.

"Congratulations! You've won a $500 Amazon gift card. Claim it now!"
💡

Curiosity

Teasing with intriguing or alarming info to compel someone to click and learn more.

"Someone shared embarrassing photos of you. See them here."

Real-World Examples

CRITICAL
2020

🐦 Twitter Bitcoin Scam

Hackers used spear phishing on Twitter employees, gaining access to internal admin tools. They compromised accounts of Elon Musk, Barack Obama, Apple, and more — posting Bitcoin scam tweets.

💰 $120,000+ stolen 👥 130 accounts compromised 📉 Stock price dropped
CRITICAL
2016

🏠 FACC CEO Fraud

Austrian aerospace company FACC lost €42 million when an employee transferred funds after receiving a phishing email impersonating the CEO requesting an "acquisition payment."

💰 €42 million lost 👤 CEO & CFO fired 📉 Company near-bankruptcy
HIGH
2016

📧 John Podesta Email Hack

A single phishing email disguised as a Google security alert tricked a political campaign chairman into entering his password on a fake page. Led to one of the most significant data leaks in U.S. political history.

📄 50,000+ emails leaked 🌍 Global political impact
HIGH
2023

🏢 MGM Resorts Attack

Attackers used vishing (voice phishing) — a simple phone call to the IT help desk impersonating an employee — to gain access. Result: systems down for 10 days, $100M+ in losses.

💰 $100M+ losses 🏨 10 days of outages 🎰 Casinos disrupted
💡
Key Takeaway: Phishing attacks don't just target individuals — they can bring down entire organizations. Even tech-savvy people and major corporations fall victim. Awareness is your strongest defense.
07

Best Practices & Tips

🛡️ Prevention

1
Enable Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA)

Even if your password is stolen, MFA adds another layer that blocks unauthorized access.

2
Use a Password Manager

Generates unique, strong passwords for every site. A password manager won't auto-fill on fake domains.

3
Keep Software Updated

Security patches fix known vulnerabilities that phishing attacks might exploit.

🔎 Detection

4
Verify Through Separate Channels

Got a suspicious email from your boss? Call or message them directly to confirm — don't reply to the email.

5
Inspect URLs Before Clicking

Hover over links to see the real destination. Type URLs directly in the browser instead of clicking links.

6
Be Skeptical of Urgency

Legitimate companies rarely demand immediate action via email. Take a moment to verify before acting.

🚨 Response

7
Report Suspicious Emails

Use your email client's "Report Phishing" button. Notify your IT/security team immediately.

8
If You Clicked — Act Fast

Change your password immediately, enable MFA, notify IT, and monitor your accounts for unusual activity.
